Our Phased Mission

A strategic, two-phase approach to build a sustainable fusion enterprise.

Phase 1: Medical Isotope Production

Address the critical shortage of medical isotopes like Molybdenum-99 by developing a compact, efficient fusion device. This generates early revenue and validates our core technology in a vital, regulated market.

Phase 2: Grid-Scale Fusion Power

Leverage our proven technology, operational expertise, and established supply chains to build and commercialize fusion power plants. Our goal is to provide clean, safe, and limitless energy for India and the world.

Core Technology

Our innovation lies in a novel approach to fusion, optimized for efficiency and commercial viability.

Advanced GDT Device

Utilizing a Gas-Dynamic Trap (GDT) configuration, which offers superior plasma stability and a simpler, more linear design compared to complex toroidal systems.

High-Efficiency Neutron Source

Our device is designed to be a powerful neutron source, making it ideal for breeding medical isotopes and testing materials for future power plants.

Modular & Scalable

The linear architecture allows for modular construction and scalability, reducing construction time and enabling incremental upgrades.

A Legacy of Innovation

Fusion energy is built on nearly a century of scientific breakthroughs and relentless engineering.

1920s-1930s: The Dawn of an Idea

Arthur Eddington proposes that stars are powered by fusing hydrogen into helium. Hans Bethe later details the proton-proton chain, the specific fusion process in stars, earning a Nobel Prize.

1950s: The First Machines

The Cold War accelerates fusion research. The "Tokamak" concept is developed in the Soviet Union, and the "Stellarator" at Princeton. The first fusion neutrons are produced in a lab.

1980s-1990s: Major Breakthroughs

The Joint European Torus (JET) in the UK achieves the world's first controlled release of fusion power in 1991, followed by significant power output from TFTR at Princeton in 1994.

2000s-Present: The Age of Collaboration & Commercialization

The international ITER project begins construction in France, aiming to demonstrate a burning plasma. Simultaneously, a vibrant private fusion industry emerges, with companies like ASPL Fusion pioneering new, commercially-focused approaches.

Our Roadmap

From critical near-term applications to long-term energy independence.

Year 1-2: R&D and Prototyping

Finalize physics design, build and test the initial low-beta GDT prototype. Secure seed funding and key partnerships.

Year 3-4: Isotope Production Validation

Construct a full-scale device capable of producing commercially viable quantities of Mo-99. Begin regulatory approval process.

Year 5-7: Commercial Isotope Sales

Achieve first revenue through sales of medical isotopes to hospitals and pharmaceutical companies, establishing a self-sustaining business model.

Year 8+: Fusion Power Development

Re-invest revenue into R&D for a net-energy-gain fusion device. Design and build India's first commercial fusion power demonstrator.
