Simplicity by Design: The Advanced Magnetic Mirror

We are focusing on advanced linear magnetic mirror systems, a technology path known for its high plasma stability. This approach avoids the complexities of toroidal tokamaks, making it simpler to build, maintain, and scale for commercial power plants. Recent breakthroughs in High-Temperature Superconducting (HTS) magnets make this the superior architecture for reliable, economical fusion power.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about fusion energy and our approach.

Fusion is the process that powers the sun and stars. It occurs when atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy. Unlike nuclear fission, fusion produces no long-lived radioactive waste and uses fuel derived from water, making it virtually limitless and domestically available.

Our advanced magnetic mirror technology creates the extreme conditions needed for fusion by using powerful magnetic fields to confine and heat plasma to temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees Celsius.

We focus on a phased, de-risked approach: first solving India's medical isotope needs, then scaling to grid power. Our technology is modular, scalable, and designed for commercial viability from day one.

Unlike tokamak-based approaches, our linear magnetic mirror system is simpler to build, maintain, and scale. We're also targeting adjacent markets first to generate early revenue and validate our technology before pursuing grid-scale power.

Medical isotope production provides an ideal entry point for several reasons:

  • High-value market with stable demand
  • Validates our core technology in a regulated environment
  • Generates early revenue to fund further development
  • Serves critical national healthcare needs
  • Builds operational expertise for future power plants

A Legacy of Innovation

Fusion energy is built on nearly a century of scientific breakthroughs and relentless engineering.

1920s-1930s: The Dawn of an Idea

Arthur Eddington proposes that stars are powered by fusing hydrogen into helium. Hans Bethe later details the proton-proton chain, the specific fusion process in stars, earning a Nobel Prize.

1950s: The First Machines

The Cold War accelerates fusion research. The "Tokamak" concept is developed in the Soviet Union, and the "Stellarator" at Princeton. The first fusion neutrons are produced in a lab.

1980s-1990s: Major Breakthroughs

The Joint European Torus (JET) in the UK achieves the world's first controlled release of fusion power in 1991, followed by significant power output from TFTR at Princeton in 1994.

2000s-Present: The Age of Collaboration & Commercialization

The international ITER project begins construction in France, aiming to demonstrate a burning plasma. Simultaneously, a vibrant private fusion industry emerges, with companies like ASPL Fusion pioneering new, commercially-focused approaches.

Our Roadmap

From critical near-term applications to long-term energy independence.

Year 1-2: R&D and Prototyping

Finalize physics design, build and test the initial low-beta GDT prototype. Secure seed funding and key partnerships.

Year 3-4: Isotope Production Validation

Construct a full-scale device capable of producing commercially viable quantities of Mo-99. Begin regulatory approval process.

Year 5-7: Commercial Isotope Sales

Achieve first revenue through sales of medical isotopes to hospitals and pharmaceutical companies, establishing a self-sustaining business model.

Year 8+: Fusion Power Development

Re-invest revenue into R&D for a net-energy-gain fusion device. Design and build India's first commercial fusion power demonstrator.
